# 1. Brand & Visual Identity Analysis

Based on the Yashvini Skin & Hair Clinic name and dermatology positioning, the brand should feel clean, premium, and medically trustworthy while remaining warm and approachable.

# 2. Target Audience Research

## Primary Patient Segments

---

### Segment 1: Telugu-Speaking Local Patients (Core Audience)

**Demographics:** Age 25–60, primarily local city residents, moderate to upper-middle income
**Language:** Telugu primary, limited English comfort
**Search behavior:** Voice search in Telugu, Google in transliterated Telugu or English
**Common searches:** "skin doctor near me", "జుట్టు రాలడం treatment", "pimple treatment clinic"
**Pain points:**
- Difficulty understanding English medical content
- Distrust of online medical information
- Prefer doctor recommendations from community/family
- Cultural stigma around skin conditions (vitiligo, acne scarring)

**Cultural considerations:**
- Marriage-driven urgency for skin treatments (pigmentation, acne scars before weddings)
- Festival skin care needs (pre-Ugadi, Diwali)
- Joint family decision-making — content must appeal to both patient and family
- Trust signals: doctor's educational qualifications shown prominently (MD, MBBS, DVL)

---

### Segment 2: Young Urban Professionals (22–35)

**Language:** English-first, Telugu secondary
**Concerns:** Acne, acne scars, pigmentation, early aging signs, laser hair removal
**Search behavior:** Google, Instagram, YouTube, Reddit
**Booking behavior:** Online booking, WhatsApp preferred over phone calls
**Content needs:** Before/after results, treatment timelines, pricing transparency
**Pain points:** Time-poor, want quick effective solutions, value evidence-based treatments

---

### Segment 3: Women Seeking Aesthetic Treatments (25–45)

**Key concerns:** Melasma, skin brightening, anti-aging, facial hair removal, HydraFacial, chemical peels
**Triggers:** Weddings (own or family), pregnancy-related skin changes, returning to work post-maternity
**Decision factors:** Doctor credentials, clinic hygiene, before/after photos, word-of-mouth
**Content needs:** Detailed procedure explanations, expected results, downtime information

---

### Segment 4: Teenagers with Acne (13–19)

**Brought by parents, but also self-research via Instagram/YouTube**
**Concerns:** Active acne, blackheads, whiteheads, acne marks
**Pain points:** Social anxiety, school/college confidence
**Content strategy:** Parent-facing educational content + teen-friendly explainer content
**Keywords:** "pimple treatment for teenagers", "acne doctor for students"

---

### Segment 5: Men with Hair Loss (25–50)

**Fastest growing segment in India**
**Concerns:** Receding hairline, crown thinning, beard thinning, dandruff
**Search behavior:** YouTube research heavy, seek before/after proof
**Content needs:** PRP results, medication options, realistic expectations, cost
**Cultural note:** Hair loss stigma is high — content must be empathetic and non-judgmental
**Keywords:** "hair fall solution for men", "PRP treatment cost", "hair transplant vs PRP"

---

### Segment 6: Patients with Chronic Skin Conditions (All Ages)

**Conditions:** Eczema, psoriasis, vitiligo, chronic urticaria, rosacea
**Behavior:** Long search journey, compare multiple clinics, need detailed medical content
**Pain points:** Failed home remedies, disappointment with previous treatments
**Content needs:** Honest condition explanations, treatment options, management (not always cure)
**Trust builder:** Doctor's expertise in chronic conditions explicitly stated

# 4. GEO + AEO Optimization Strategy

## AEO — Answer Engine Optimization

AEO targets Google's Featured Snippets, People Also Ask (PAA), and voice search results.

### FAQ Content Framework

Every treatment page must include a structured FAQ section with:
- 8–12 questions per page
- Answers between 40–60 words (ideal for featured snippets)
- Schema-marked with `FAQPage` JSON-LD
- Questions written in natural language (as patients would ask)

### Example FAQ Structure (Acne Treatment Page)

```
Q: What is the best treatment for acne in adults?
A: Adult acne is treated based on severity. Mild acne responds to topical retinoids
and benzoyl peroxide. Moderate acne may require oral antibiotics or hormonal therapy.
Severe acne often needs isotretinoin prescribed by a dermatologist after proper evaluation.

Q: How long does acne treatment take to show results?
A: Most acne treatments show initial improvement in 4–6 weeks. Complete clearance
may take 3–6 months depending on the type and severity of acne. Consistency with
prescribed treatment is key to long-term results.
```

### People Also Ask (PAA) Targeting

Research PAA boxes for each target keyword and write direct answers. Common PAA patterns:
- "How long does [treatment] last?"
- "Is [treatment] painful?"
- "How much does [treatment] cost in India?"
- "What are the side effects of [treatment]?"
- "Can [condition] be cured permanently?"

4. **E-E-A-T signals** — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness

# 5. Dermatology & Hair Treatment Research

## SKIN CONDITIONS

---

### 5.1 Acne (మొటిమలు)

**Cause:** Excess sebum production, clogged pores (Propionibacterium acnes bacteria), hormonal fluctuations (androgens), diet, stress
**Symptoms:** Blackheads, whiteheads, papules, pustules, nodules, cysts; commonly on face, chest, back
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical: Benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid, retinoids, clindamycin
- Oral: Antibiotics (doxycycline), isotretinoin, hormonal therapy (females)
- Procedures: Chemical peels (salicylic, glycolic), comedone extraction, laser (PDT, 1450nm diode)
- Post-acne: Microneedling, fractional laser, chemical peels for scars

**When to See a Dermatologist:** When OTC products fail after 8 weeks, when cysts/nodules appear, when significant scarring begins
**Home Care:** Non-comedogenic moisturizer, SPF daily, no picking/squeezing, gentle face wash (twice daily)
**Prevention:** Low-glycemic diet, stress management, regular face washing after sweating, change pillowcases weekly

---

### 5.2 Pigmentation & Hyperpigmentation (చర్మ మచ్చలు)

**Cause:** Excess melanin production; triggered by sun exposure, hormones, inflammation, medications
**Types:** Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH), solar lentigines, melasma, freckles
**Symptoms:** Dark patches, uneven skin tone, spots on face/arms/neck
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical: Hydroquinone, kojic acid, niacinamide, azelaic acid, tranexamic acid, vitamin C serums
- Procedures: Chemical peels (Jessner's, TCA), Q-switched Nd:YAG laser, IPL, microdermabrasion
- Sunscreen: SPF 50+ mandatory (PA++++ for India's UV levels)

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Patches expanding, asymmetric or changing appearance (rule out melanoma), pregnancy-related pigmentation
**Home Care:** Strict sun protection, vitamin C serum, avoid direct sun 10am–4pm
**Prevention:** Daily broad-spectrum sunscreen, reapply every 2–3 hours, protective clothing

---

### 5.3 Melasma (మెలాస్మా)

**Cause:** Hormonal triggers (pregnancy, oral contraceptives) + UV exposure; genetic predisposition; common in Fitzpatrick III–V skin types
**Symptoms:** Symmetric brown/grayish-brown patches on cheeks, forehead, upper lip, chin
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical triple combination (hydroquinone + tretinoin + steroid) — short-term use
- Tranexamic acid (oral or topical)
- Chemical peels — superficial, done in series
- Low-fluence Q-switched laser (carefully)
- Strict photoprotection

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Any new or worsening facial pigmentation, especially during or post-pregnancy
**Home Care:** SPF 50+ PA++++, physical sun protection (hat, umbrella), avoid heat triggers
**Prevention:** Sun avoidance, treat hormonal triggers, maintain results with maintenance therapy

---

### 5.4 Eczema / Atopic Dermatitis (చర్మ వ్యాధి)

**Cause:** Impaired skin barrier + immune dysregulation; genetic (FLG gene mutations); environmental triggers: dust mites, pet dander, fragrances, certain foods
**Symptoms:** Intense itching, dry red patches, oozing/crusting (acute), thickened skin (chronic); inner elbows, behind knees, face
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical corticosteroids (short-term)
- Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus)
- Moisturizers (barrier repair) — multiple times daily
- Oral antihistamines for itch
- Systemic: Dupilumab (biologic), cyclosporine, methotrexate for severe cases
- Phototherapy (NB-UVB)

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Uncontrolled itch disrupting sleep, skin infection signs (yellow crusting, fever), not responding to OTC treatments
**Home Care:** Fragrance-free moisturizer 2–3x daily, lukewarm baths, cotton clothing, identify and avoid triggers
**Prevention:** Consistent emollient use from infancy, avoid harsh soaps, maintain cool humid environment

---

### 5.5 Psoriasis (సోరియాసిస్)

**Cause:** Autoimmune — T-cell mediated accelerated skin cell turnover (cells replace in 3–4 days vs. normal 28–30 days); genetic + environmental triggers
**Symptoms:** Silver-white scaly plaques on red skin, commonly elbows, knees, scalp, lower back; can affect nails and joints (psoriatic arthritis)
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical: Corticosteroids, vitamin D analogues (calcipotriol), coal tar, tazarotene
- Phototherapy: NB-UVB, PUVA
- Systemic: Methotrexate, cyclosporine, acitretin
- Biologics: Adalimumab, secukinumab, ixekizumab (for moderate-severe)

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Any new plaque-like rash, psoriasis with joint pain, widespread disease
**Home Care:** Daily moisturizing, avoid known triggers (stress, alcohol, certain medications), no scratching
**Prevention:** Stress management, avoid skin trauma (Koebner phenomenon), maintain healthy weight

---

### 5.6 Fungal Infections (శిలీంధ్ర సంక్రమణలు)

**Very common in India due to hot, humid climate**
**Types:** Tinea corporis (ringworm), tinea cruris (groin), tinea pedis (athlete's foot), tinea versicolor (pityriasis versicolor), onychomycosis (nail)
**Cause:** Dermatophyte fungi (Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton); Malassezia (tinea versicolor)
**Symptoms:** Ring-shaped scaly patches, itching, discoloration; nails: thickened, discolored, brittle
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical antifungals: Clotrimazole, miconazole, terbinafine, luliconazole
- Oral antifungals: Fluconazole, itraconazole, terbinafine (for resistant or nail cases)
- **Warning:** Steroid-antifungal combinations widely misused in India — leads to Tinea incognito

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Spreading infection, nail involvement, not responding to OTC treatments
**Home Care:** Keep skin dry, change clothes after sweating, breathable cotton clothing, avoid sharing towels
**Prevention:** Proper hygiene, moisture control, treat family members to prevent spread

---

### 5.7 Vitiligo (తెల్ల మచ్చలు)

**Cause:** Autoimmune destruction of melanocytes; genetic predisposition; associated with thyroid disorders
**Symptoms:** Depigmented white patches anywhere on body; can affect hair within patches
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical: Tacrolimus, corticosteroids, calcipotriol
- Phototherapy: NB-UVB (first-line for widespread vitiligo)
- Oral: JAK inhibitors (ruxolitinib — recent approval), minocycline
- Surgical: Melanocyte transplantation, punch grafting (stable vitiligo)
- Cosmetic: Camouflage creams for social occasions

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Any new white patches — early treatment gives best results
**Psychosocial note:** Significant psychological impact especially in South Indian society — counseling support important
**Home Care:** Sun protection of depigmented areas (no melanin = burns easily), vitamin D supplementation

---

### 5.8 Rosacea

**Cause:** Exact mechanism unclear; involves neurovascular dysfunction + immune response; Demodex mites role; genetic
**Symptoms:** Facial redness/flushing, visible blood vessels, bumps/pimples, eye involvement (ocular rosacea)
**Triggers:** Spicy food, hot beverages, alcohol, sun exposure, temperature extremes, stress
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical: Metronidazole, azelaic acid, ivermectin, brimonidine (for redness)
- Oral: Doxycycline (anti-inflammatory dose), isotretinoin for severe papulopustular
- Laser/IPL: For persistent redness and telangiectasias

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Persistent facial redness, eye symptoms, worsening symptoms
**Home Care:** Gentle skincare routine, mineral sunscreen (no chemical UV filters), avoid known triggers, lukewarm water

---

### 5.9 Skin Allergies & Urticaria (చర్మ అలర్జీ)

**Cause:** Allergic contact dermatitis (metals, fragrances, preservatives); irritant contact dermatitis; urticaria (hives) from food, medications, infections
**Symptoms:** Hives (raised, itchy wheals), redness, swelling, blistering; may be localized or widespread
**Treatment Options:**
- Antihistamines (cetirizine, fexofenadine, bilastine)
- Topical/oral corticosteroids for acute flares
- Patch testing to identify allergens
- Omalizumab for chronic spontaneous urticaria
- Avoidance of identified triggers

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Recurring hives, angioedema (face/throat swelling), anaphylaxis — seek emergency care immediately
**Home Care:** Identify and avoid triggers, cool compresses for relief, non-soap cleansers

---

### 5.10 Warts & Skin Tags

**Warts:** Caused by HPV (human papillomavirus); common, plantar, filiform, flat types
**Skin Tags (Acrochordons):** Benign outgrowths, common in skin folds; associated with insulin resistance/obesity
**Treatment:**
- Warts: Cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen), salicylic acid, electrocautery, laser
- Skin Tags: Electrocautery, cryotherapy, scissor excision under local anesthesia
**When to See a Dermatologist:** Multiple or rapidly spreading warts, painful plantar warts, skin tags in visible areas

## HAIR CONDITIONS

---

### 5.11 Hair Fall / Hair Loss (జుట్టు రాలడం)

**Cause:** Nutritional deficiency (iron, vitamin D, zinc, B12), hormonal imbalance, thyroid disorders, stress, post-partum, medication side effects
**Symptoms:** Increased hair on comb/pillow, widening part line, ponytail thinning
**Treatment Options:**
- Address underlying cause (blood tests essential: CBC, ferritin, thyroid, vitamin D)
- Oral: Iron supplements, biotin, multivitamin targeted for hair
- Topical minoxidil (2–5%)
- PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma) therapy
- Nutritional counseling

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Losing >100 hairs/day for more than 3 months, sudden dramatic loss, bald patches
**Home Care:** Gentle sulfate-free shampoo, avoid tight hairstyles, reduce heat styling, balanced nutrition

---

### 5.12 Androgenetic Alopecia (Genetic Hair Loss) (వంశపారంపర్య జుట్టు రాలడం)

**Most common cause of hair loss — affects 50% of men by age 50, 25% of women**
**Cause:** DHT (dihydrotestosterone) sensitivity in genetically predisposed hair follicles → miniaturization
**Pattern:** Men — hairline recession + crown thinning (Hamilton-Norwood scale); Women — diffuse thinning over crown (Ludwig scale)
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical minoxidil (men: 5%, women: 2–5%)
- Oral finasteride (men), dutasteride
- Oral minoxidil (low dose — growing evidence)
- PRP therapy (maintenance and enhancement)
- Low-Level Laser Therapy (LLLT)
- Hair transplant (FUE/FUT) for advanced cases

**When to See a Dermatologist:** As early as possible — earlier treatment = better outcome

---

### 5.13 Dandruff & Seborrheic Dermatitis (పొలుసు)

**Cause:** Malassezia yeast overgrowth on oily skin areas; sebaceous gland activity; immune response
**Symptoms:** White/yellow flakes on scalp and shoulders, scalp itching, oily scalp; in seborrheic dermatitis — red scaly patches on scalp, face (eyebrows, nasolabial folds)
**Treatment Options:**
- Medicated shampoos: Ketoconazole, zinc pyrithione, selenium sulfide, coal tar, ciclopirox
- Topical corticosteroids for inflammation (scalp lotion or mousse)
- Maintenance: Twice-weekly medicated shampoo

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Severe dandruff with hair loss, spreading to face, not responding to OTC shampoos
**Home Care:** Regular scalp hygiene, avoid heavy oily hair products, manage stress

---

### 5.14 Alopecia Areata (వృత్తాకార జుట్టు రాలడం)

**Cause:** Autoimmune — immune system attacks hair follicles; associated with thyroid, vitiligo, Down syndrome
**Symptoms:** Sudden, well-defined round bald patches; scalp, beard, eyebrows, eyelashes; no scarring
**Treatment Options:**
- Topical/intralesional corticosteroids (first-line)
- Topical minoxidil (supportive)
- Topical immunotherapy (DPCP)
- Oral JAK inhibitors (baricitinib — FDA approved for severe AA)
- Phototherapy (PUVA, NB-UVB)

**When to See a Dermatologist:** Any bald patches — autoimmune workup needed
**Psychological impact:** Significant; counseling referral important

---

### 5.15 Telogen Effluvium

**Cause:** Excessive hair shedding 2–3 months after a triggering event: fever/illness (including COVID-19), surgery, crash dieting, childbirth, severe stress, thyroid changes
**Symptoms:** Diffuse shedding, positive hair pull test, no bald patches
**Treatment:** Address underlying trigger; nutritional supplementation; reassurance (usually self-limiting in 6–9 months)
**When to See a Dermatologist:** If shedding continues beyond 6 months (rule out chronic telogen effluvium or AGA)

---

## COSMETOLOGY TREATMENTS

---

### 5.16 Chemical Peels

**What it is:** Controlled application of chemical solution to exfoliate skin layers and stimulate collagen
**Types:** Superficial (glycolic, salicylic, lactic), medium-depth (TCA 15–35%, Jessner's), deep (phenol — clinic-only)
**Indications:** Acne, post-acne scars, pigmentation, melasma, uneven skin tone, dull skin, fine lines
**Procedure:** 20–30 minutes; peeling occurs 3–7 days post-procedure
**Sessions:** 4–6 sessions, 3–4 weeks apart (superficial); 1–2 sessions (medium)
**Downtime:** Superficial — minimal; Medium — 5–7 days skin peeling
**Precautions:** Strict sun avoidance post-peel; SPF mandatory; avoid actives 1 week pre/post

---

### 5.17 Laser Hair Removal (లేజర్ హెయిర్ రిమూవల్)

**Technology:** Diode laser (800–810nm) — gold standard for Indian skin tones
**How it works:** Laser targets melanin in hair follicle → heat destroys follicle
**Areas:** Upper lip, underarms, bikini, legs, back, arms, face
**Sessions required:** 6–8 sessions (spaced 4–6 weeks apart) for 70–90% permanent reduction
**Skin type consideration:** Fitzpatrick III–V (most South Indians) — requires experienced operator, appropriate laser settings, cooling system
**Post-care:** Avoid sun, no heat (sauna, hot water) 48h, SPF daily
**Realistic outcome:** Permanent hair reduction (not complete removal); maintenance sessions may be needed

---

### 5.18 PRP Hair Therapy (PRP హెయిర్ థెరపీ)

**What it is:** Platelet-Rich Plasma — patient's own blood processed to concentrate growth factors; injected into scalp
**Mechanism:** Growth factors (PDGF, VEGF, EGF) stimulate dormant follicles, increase blood supply, prolong anagen phase
**Indications:** Androgenetic alopecia, hair thinning, alopecia areata (adjunct), post-transplant maintenance
**Sessions:** 3–4 initial sessions (4 weeks apart); maintenance every 3–6 months
**Procedure:** Blood draw → centrifuge → scalp injections → 45–60 minutes
**Results:** Expected in 3–6 months; improved hair density, reduced shedding
**Not a cure** — maintenance required for ongoing results

---

### 5.19 Microneedling (Collagen Induction Therapy)

**What it is:** Controlled micro-injuries using fine needles → triggers wound healing → new collagen and elastin
**Indications:** Acne scars (rolling, boxcar), enlarged pores, fine lines, skin texture, stretch marks, skin rejuvenation
**Variants:** Dermapen (automated), dermaroller, RF microneedling (adds radiofrequency for enhanced tightening)
**Sessions:** 4–6 sessions, 4 weeks apart
**Downtime:** 24–48h redness (mild)
**Combined with:** PRP (PRP + microneedling = enhanced results), hyaluronic acid serums

---

### 5.20 HydraFacial

**What it is:** Multi-step skin treatment using a patented device: cleanse + exfoliate + extract + hydrate + protect
**Steps:** Cleanse/Peel → Acid Peel → Extractions (painless) → Hyaluronic acid infusion → Antioxidant protection
**Indications:** Dull skin, dehydration, fine lines, uneven texture, mild acne, blackheads
**Sessions:** Monthly maintenance recommended
**Downtime:** None — a "lunchtime treatment"
**Popular for:** Pre-wedding glow, skin maintenance, first-time aesthetic patients

---

### 5.21 Skin Brightening Treatments

**Approach:** Combination of topical agents + procedures
**Topical actives:** Vitamin C, niacinamide, kojic acid, arbutin, tranexamic acid
**Procedures:** Chemical peels, Q-switched Nd:YAG laser (toning mode), HydraFacial, meso-brightening
**Important:** "Brightening" = even skin tone + radiance; clinically distinct from skin lightening (bleaching)
**Ethical note:** Position as "even skin tone" and "radiance" — not fair skin promotion

---

### 5.22 Scar Treatment

**Acne Scars:** Classified as atrophic (ice-pick, boxcar, rolling) or hypertrophic/keloidal
**Treatments by scar type:**
- Rolling scars: Subcision + filler, RF microneedling
- Boxcar scars: Fractional CO2 laser, TCA CROSS
- Ice-pick scars: TCA CROSS, punch excision
- Hypertrophic/keloid: Intralesional steroids, silicone gel, laser

**Realistic expectations:** 40–70% improvement typically achievable; multiple sessions required

---

### 5.23 Anti-Aging Treatments

**Non-invasive options:**
- Chemical peels (superficial-medium)
- Microneedling / RF microneedling
- HIFU (High-Intensity Focused Ultrasound) — skin lifting
- PRP facial (vampire facial)
- Mesotherapy (vitamins + hyaluronic acid injections)
- LED light therapy

**Injectable options (if doctor trained and licensed):**
- Botulinum toxin (Botox) — dynamic wrinkles, forehead lines, crow's feet
- Dermal fillers (hyaluronic acid) — volume restoration, lip enhancement, nasolabial folds

# 9. Legal & Ethical Compliance

## Ethical Dermatology Marketing Guidelines

### Permitted Marketing Claims
- Treatment options available (not guaranteed results)
- Doctor credentials and qualifications
- Equipment and technology used
- General patient satisfaction (via reviews — authentic only)
- Educational content about conditions

### Prohibited / Restricted Content
- Claims of "permanent cure" for chronic conditions (psoriasis, vitiligo, rosacea)
- "100% results" or guarantee language
- Misleading before/after comparisons (different lighting, makeup, angles)
- Testimonials that imply all patients get same results
- Exaggerated claims about non-medical treatments

---

## Medical Disclaimer (Required on Website)

Include the following (or legally reviewed equivalent) in footer and on treatment pages:

> **Medical Disclaimer:** The content on this website is for informational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always consult a qualified dermatologist for any skin or hair concerns. Results of treatments vary from patient to patient. Before/after images shown represent individual outcomes and are not a guarantee of results for all patients. Consult your treating doctor for personalized recommendations.

---

## Before/After Image Regulations

Per Indian Medical Council guidelines and global best practices:

- **Mandatory:** Written informed consent from patient before publishing any image
- **Mandatory:** Consistent lighting, angle, distance, no makeup in comparison photos
- **Mandatory:** Watermark with clinic name on all before/after images
- **Mandatory:** Caption: "Results may vary. Individual patient result."
- **Prohibited:** Manipulated or filtered photos
- **Prohibited:** Images without patient consent
- **Best practice:** Include timeframe (e.g., "Before and 3 months after 4 sessions of Chemical Peel")
- **Storage:** Patient consent records must be maintained in clinic records

---

## Patient Data Protection

- Collect only necessary data via appointment forms
- Use SSL/HTTPS throughout website
- Include Privacy Policy page
- Comply with India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA) 2023
- Do not share patient data with third parties without consent
- WhatsApp communications — patients should consent to WhatsApp consultation

---

## Advertising Standards Council of India (ASCI) Guidelines

Healthcare advertising in India must:
- Not promote prescription drugs directly to patients
- Not make false or misleading health claims
- Include "Consult your doctor" disclaimers where appropriate
- Not exploit patient insecurities (body image, skin color)

### Cross-linking Rules
- Each treatment page links to: related condition page + 2 related treatment pages + contact page
- Blog posts link to: relevant treatment page (1–2 links per post)
- FAQ page links to relevant treatment pages for each answer
- Homepage links to all main hub pages

### Anchor Text Strategy
- Use descriptive, keyword-rich anchor text
- Vary anchor text (not exact same phrase every time)
- Avoid "click here" or "read more" as anchor text
