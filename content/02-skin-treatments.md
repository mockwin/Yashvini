### H2: What Is Acne?

Acne vulgaris is one of the most common skin conditions in the world, affecting people of all ages — though it is most prevalent during the teenage and young adult years. It occurs when hair follicles become clogged with oil (sebum) and dead skin cells, leading to the formation of whiteheads, blackheads, pimples, nodules, or cysts.

Contrary to popular belief, acne is not simply a hygiene problem, nor is it caused exclusively by oily food. It is a complex dermatological condition with multiple contributing factors, and it responds best to evidence-based medical treatment — not home remedies or over-the-counter fairness creams.

At Yashvini Skin & Hair Clinic, we take acne seriously because we understand how deeply it can affect your confidence, mental health, and quality of life.

---

### H2: What Causes Acne?

Acne develops due to a combination of factors:

- **Excess sebum production:** Overactive oil glands create a favourable environment for acne-causing bacteria.
- **Hormonal changes:** Androgens (male hormones present in both males and females) stimulate oil glands. This is why acne is common during puberty, menstruation, pregnancy, and with conditions like PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome).
- **Bacterial overgrowth:** Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes) is the main bacterium involved in inflamed acne lesions.
- **Follicular hyperkeratinisation:** When skin cells inside the follicle shed abnormally and clog the pore.
- **Diet:** High glycaemic index foods and dairy products may worsen acne in some individuals.
- **Stress:** Stress hormones can trigger or worsen breakouts.
- **Skincare products:** Using heavy, comedogenic (pore-clogging) products can contribute to acne.
- **Medications:** Certain medicines, including steroids and some birth control pills, can cause acne.
- **Genetic factors:** A family history of acne may increase your predisposition.

---

### H2: Signs & Symptoms of Acne

Acne can present in several forms, ranging from mild to severe:

- **Comedones:** Blackheads (open comedones) and whiteheads (closed comedones) — non-inflamed, clogged pores
- **Papules:** Small, red, raised bumps that may be tender to touch
- **Pustules:** Pimples filled with pus (yellowish or white head)
- **Nodules:** Large, hard, painful lumps deep within the skin
- **Cysts:** Painful, pus-filled lumps that can cause scarring
- **Scarring and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH):** Dark spots and textural changes left after acne heals

Common locations: Face (especially forehead, nose, chin — the T-zone), back, chest, shoulders.

### H1: Acne Scar Treatment in Nandyala — Reduce Scars, Restore Confidence

### H2: What Are Acne Scars?

Acne scars are permanent or long-lasting textural and pigmentary changes that remain on the skin after acne lesions heal. They are particularly common after inflammatory acne (papules, pustules, nodules, cysts). Not all dark spots after acne are true scars — many are cases of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH), which is a discolouration that can fade with proper treatment. True acne scars involve changes in the skin's collagen structure and may be either depressed (atrophic) or raised (hypertrophic/keloid).

Understanding the type of scar you have is crucial, as different scars respond to different treatments. At Yashvini Skin & Hair Clinic, we carefully classify your scars before recommending any procedure.

---

### H2: Types of Acne Scars

- **Icepick scars:** Deep, narrow, pitted scars that extend into the dermis. Named for their sharp, pin-hole appearance.
- **Boxcar scars:** Broad, rectangular depressions with sharp, well-defined edges.
- **Rolling scars:** Wide, shallow scars with sloping edges that give skin an undulating, wave-like appearance.
- **Hypertrophic scars:** Raised, firm scars that remain within the boundary of the original acne lesion.
- **Keloid scars:** Raised scars that grow beyond the original lesion boundary — more common in darker skin tones.
- **Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH):** Flat, dark spots — not true scars but a common concern after acne.

---

### H2: Causes of Acne Scarring

Scarring occurs when acne lesions damage the deeper layers of the skin (dermis) and the healing process produces abnormal amounts of collagen. Factors that increase scarring risk:

- Delayed or inadequate treatment of inflammatory acne
- Picking, squeezing, or popping pimples (significantly increases risk)
- Severe acne grades (nodules and cysts are more likely to scar)
- Genetic predisposition
- Darker skin tones (more prone to PIH and keloid scars)

---

### H2: Acne Scar Treatments at Yashvini Clinic

#### H3: Microneedling (Collagen Induction Therapy)
Medical microneedling uses fine needles to create controlled micro-injuries in the skin, stimulating collagen production and skin remodelling. It is effective for atrophic scars (icepick, boxcar, rolling) and improves skin texture and tone. Multiple sessions are typically required.

#### H3: Chemical Peels for Scars
Medium-depth peels (such as TCA peels) penetrate deeper into the skin to encourage cellular renewal, reduce PIH, and improve the appearance of superficial scars. Peel strength is selected based on your scar type and skin tone.

#### H3: Dermal Fillers (for Rolling/Boxcar Scars)
Hyaluronic acid or other appropriate fillers may be used to temporarily fill depressed scars, improving surface contour. This is a short-to-medium-term solution.

#### H3: Subcision
A minor surgical procedure where a needle is inserted under the skin to break up fibrous scar tissue that tethers rolling scars to deeper layers. Often combined with microneedling or fillers.

#### H3: Laser Resurfacing
Fractional laser treatments resurface the skin and stimulate collagen production, effective for improving scar texture. Laser selection is carefully matched to skin tone to minimise risks.

#### H3: Topical Treatments
Prescription topicals containing retinoids, vitamin C, niacinamide, and hydroquinone (where appropriate) help address PIH and support overall skin renewal.

Complete removal of deep structural scars is rarely possible. However, appropriate treatment can significantly reduce their appearance — often by 50–80% — making them much less noticeable. The goal is visible, meaningful improvement.

Temporary side effects may include redness, swelling, peeling, and mild darkening of scars before improvement. More serious complications are rare when procedures are performed by a qualified dermatologist. Risk of PIH is higher in darker skin tones without proper pre-treatment protocol.

**Q8: Is it safe to get scar treatment while I still have active acne?**
Active, inflamed acne should generally be controlled before undergoing scar treatments. Your doctor will assess and sequence your treatment plan appropriately.

### H1: Pigmentation Treatment in Nandyala — Even Out Your Skin Tone Safely

### H2: What Is Skin Pigmentation?

Skin pigmentation refers to the colour of your skin, which is determined by a pigment called melanin produced by cells called melanocytes. When these cells are damaged or produce excess melanin, it results in areas of discolouration — appearing as dark patches, spots, or uneven skin tone. This is known as hyperpigmentation.

In India, pigmentation is one of the most common skin concerns — made more challenging by our tropical climate, high UV exposure, and the fact that Indian skin types are genetically more prone to melanin overproduction in response to triggers such as sun exposure, inflammation, hormones, and injury.

---

### H2: Types of Pigmentation

- **Sunspots / Solar lentigines:** Flat dark spots caused by long-term UV exposure. Common on the face, hands, and forearms.
- **Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH):** Dark marks left after acne, eczema, insect bites, or other skin injury.
- **Melasma:** Symmetrical dark patches often related to hormonal changes. Covered in detail on our Melasma Treatment page.
- **Freckles (ephelides):** Small, flat brown spots — often genetic, worsened by sun.
- **Periorbital (under-eye) hyperpigmentation:** Dark circles under the eyes, which may have multiple causes.

---

### H2: Causes of Pigmentation

- Prolonged sun exposure without sunscreen (the single biggest cause)
- Hormonal changes (pregnancy, contraceptive pills, thyroid disorders)
- Skin inflammation from acne, eczema, or injury
- Friction (from tight clothing, jewellery)
- Certain medications (some antibiotics, chemotherapy drugs, antipsychotics)
- Nutritional deficiencies (Vitamin B12, Vitamin D)
- Genetic predisposition

---

### H2: Pigmentation Symptoms

- Dark spots or patches on the face, neck, arms, or body
- Uneven skin tone — some areas clearly darker than others
- Skin that looks dull or lacklustre
- Patches that worsen with sun exposure

---

### H2: Pigmentation Treatments at Yashvini Clinic

#### H3: Prescription Topical Agents
A medically supervised skincare regimen is the cornerstone of pigmentation treatment. This typically includes combinations of depigmenting agents such as hydroquinone, kojic acid, arbutin, niacinamide, retinoids, and vitamin C — chosen based on pigmentation type and skin sensitivity.

#### H3: Chemical Peels
Superficial to medium-depth peels exfoliate the skin's surface, accelerate cell turnover, and reduce pigmentation. Commonly used agents include glycolic acid, lactic acid, kojic acid, and mandelic acid. A series of treatments is required for best results.

#### H3: Laser & Light Treatments
Q-switched Nd:YAG laser and other light-based treatments can target melanin deposits in the skin. Careful selection of laser type and parameters is essential for safe treatment of Indian skin tones to avoid worsening pigmentation.

#### H3: Microneedling with Depigmenting Serum
Combines the collagen-stimulating benefits of microneedling with the delivery of brightening serums into the skin — an effective combination for resistant pigmentation.

#### H3: Sunscreen Guidance
Consistent, correct sunscreen use is non-negotiable in pigmentation treatment. We advise patients on selecting and using SPF 50+ broad-spectrum sunscreen — the most critical step in preventing further pigmentation.

Pigmentation treatment is a gradual process. Superficial pigmentation typically responds faster (within weeks to months). Deeper pigmentation (dermal melasma) may take longer and requires more intensive treatment. Sun protection is mandatory throughout and indefinitely.

**Q1: What causes dark patches on the face?**
Dark patches on the face can be caused by sun exposure, hormonal changes, post-acne marks, friction, or conditions like melasma. A proper diagnosis by a dermatologist is important before starting any treatment.

Many forms of pigmentation can be significantly improved. However, some types — particularly hormonally driven melasma — may require long-term maintenance. Sun protection is the most important factor in preventing recurrence.

### H1: Melasma Treatment in Nandyala — Effective Management for Stubborn Facial Patches

### H2: What Is Melasma?

Melasma is a chronic skin condition characterised by symmetrical brown, greyish-brown, or dark patches on the face — most commonly on the cheeks, forehead, upper lip, nose, and chin. It is sometimes called the "mask of pregnancy" because hormonal changes during pregnancy are one of its primary triggers.

Melasma is significantly more common in women than men, and it is particularly prevalent in South Asian and South Indian populations due to genetic predisposition and high UV exposure. It is not harmful, but it can be persistent and emotionally distressing.

Importantly, melasma can be stubborn — it often reappears after treatment if sun protection is not maintained or if hormonal triggers persist. Treatment aims to manage and suppress the condition, not necessarily cure it permanently.

---

### H2: Causes & Triggers of Melasma

- **Sun exposure:** UV radiation is the most important trigger. It stimulates melanocytes to produce excess melanin.
- **Hormonal changes:** Pregnancy, oral contraceptive pills, hormone replacement therapy
- **Thyroid dysfunction:** An association has been noted between thyroid disorders and melasma
- **Genetics:** Darker skin types and a family history of melasma increase susceptibility
- **Certain medications and cosmetics:** Photosensitising drugs and harsh cosmetics can worsen melasma
- **Heat:** Heat from cooking, sunlight, and even hot water can worsen melasma beyond UV exposure alone

---

### H2: Types of Melasma

- **Epidermal melasma:** Pigment is in the superficial layers of the skin. Responds better to topical treatments.
- **Dermal melasma:** Pigment is in the deeper dermis. More difficult to treat and may appear greyish.
- **Mixed melasma:** Combination of both — most common presentation.

A Wood's lamp or dermatoscopic examination helps determine the depth of pigmentation and guides treatment selection.

---

### H2: Melasma Treatment at Yashvini Clinic

#### H3: Topical Depigmenting Regimen
A combination of prescription topicals is the first-line treatment for melasma. This typically includes hydroquinone (in appropriate concentrations), tretinoin (retinoid), kojic acid, azelaic acid, tranexamic acid, or vitamin C — combined in a regimen tailored to your skin.

#### H3: Sun Protection Protocol
Non-negotiable. SPF 50+ broad-spectrum (UVA + UVB) sunscreen must be applied every morning and reapplied throughout the day. Physical sunscreens containing zinc oxide or titanium dioxide are often preferred. Additional sun-protective measures (hat, umbrella, avoiding peak sun hours) are strongly advised.

#### H3: Chemical Peels
Carefully selected peels — including glycolic acid, lactic acid, mandelic acid, or phytic acid — help accelerate skin cell turnover and target superficial melanin deposits. Peels for melasma are approached conservatively to avoid triggering PIH.

#### H3: Oral Tranexamic Acid
An evidence-based oral medication that helps reduce melanin synthesis. Used under medical supervision as an add-on treatment for resistant or extensive melasma.

#### H3: Q-Switched Nd:YAG Laser (Low Fluence)
When used correctly and conservatively, low-fluence Q-switched Nd:YAG laser toning can help reduce melasma. This must be performed carefully in Indian skin to avoid PIH.

#### H3: Lifestyle Modifications
Avoiding heat exposure, reviewing hormonal medications with your doctor, managing thyroid health, and dietary antioxidants all contribute to better melasma control.

---

### H2: What to Expect

Melasma treatment is a gradual, ongoing process — not a quick fix. Initial improvement may be visible within 4–8 weeks of consistent topical treatment. However, melasma has a tendency to recur, particularly with sun exposure or hormonal changes. Long-term maintenance — including ongoing sun protection and periodic peel sessions — is often necessary.

### H2: Frequently Asked Questions — Melasma Treatment

**Q1: What is melasma and why does it appear on the face?**
Melasma is a form of hyperpigmentation caused by overactive melanocytes (pigment-producing cells), typically triggered by hormones and UV exposure. It appears on sun-exposed areas of the face where melanocytes are most active.

**Q2: Can melasma be permanently cured?**
Melasma is a chronic condition that can be effectively managed but may not be permanently cured. With consistent sun protection and appropriate treatment, it can be significantly suppressed. Relapse is common if triggers are not controlled.

**Q3: What is the best treatment for melasma in Indian skin?**
For Indian skin, a carefully supervised combination of topical depigmenting agents, chemical peels, oral tranexamic acid, and strict sun protection tends to give the best results. Aggressive treatments such as high-energy laser can worsen melasma in Indian skin if not carefully calibrated.

**Q4: How long does melasma treatment take?**
Visible improvement typically begins within 4–8 weeks of consistent treatment. Full results may take 3–6 months. Long-term maintenance is often required to prevent recurrence.

**Q5: Is melasma related to thyroid problems?**
A link between thyroid dysfunction and melasma has been observed. If you have melasma, your doctor may recommend thyroid function testing as part of the diagnostic workup.

**Q6: Can melasma come back after treatment?**
Yes. Melasma commonly recurs, especially with unprotected sun exposure, hormonal changes (pregnancy, new contraceptive pill), or heat exposure. This is why ongoing sun protection and maintenance treatment are so important.

**Q7: Does pregnancy cause melasma?**
Pregnancy is a common trigger for melasma due to elevated oestrogen and progesterone levels. Often called the "mask of pregnancy," it may fade after delivery but can persist. Treatment is adapted for pregnant and breastfeeding patients.

**Q8: Will chemical peels help my melasma?**
Chemical peels can be helpful for melasma, particularly for epidermal (superficial) pigmentation. However, aggressive peels can worsen melasma in some cases. At Yashvini Clinic, peel selection and strength are carefully matched to your skin type and melasma classification.

### H1: Eczema Treatment in Nandyala — Managing Atopic Dermatitis with Expert Care

### H2: What Is Eczema?

Eczema, most commonly referring to atopic dermatitis, is a chronic inflammatory skin condition characterised by dry, itchy, inflamed skin. It affects both children and adults, often beginning in infancy or early childhood. The condition tends to follow a relapsing-remitting pattern — periods of relative calm interrupted by flare-ups triggered by environmental or internal factors.

Eczema is not contagious. It is associated with a defect in the skin barrier and an overactive immune response. Patients with eczema often also have or develop asthma and allergic rhinitis — a pattern known as the "atopic march."

While there is no permanent cure for atopic dermatitis, it can be effectively managed with appropriate medical care, allowing patients to lead comfortable, active lives.

Rosacea is a chronic condition without a permanent cure, but it can be effectively managed with the right treatment and trigger avoidance. Many patients achieve long periods of remission.

Common dietary triggers include hot beverages, spicy foods, and alcohol (especially red wine). Keeping a food diary can help identify your personal triggers.

Yes. Ocular rosacea (subtype 4) affects the eyes, causing redness, dryness, and irritation. It requires separate management and may need ophthalmology co-management.

Topical and oral treatments typically show improvement within 4–8 weeks. Vascular laser treatments require multiple sessions over several months. Long-term maintenance is usually needed.

Yes. Stress and emotional flushing are well-known rosacea triggers. Stress management strategies are an important part of holistic rosacea care.

Physical/mineral sunscreens containing zinc oxide or titanium dioxide are preferred as they are less likely to cause irritation or trigger flushing compared to chemical sunscreens.

### H2: What Is Vitiligo?

Vitiligo is a chronic skin condition in which the immune system attacks and destroys melanocytes — the cells responsible for producing skin colour (melanin). This results in distinctive white or depigmented patches of skin that can appear on any part of the body.

Vitiligo affects approximately 1–2% of the population worldwide, with higher prevalence reported in India. It is not contagious, not dangerous, and not related to diet or lifestyle habits — it is an autoimmune condition. However, it can have a profound psychosocial impact, particularly in a country where skin tone carries significant social meaning.

While vitiligo cannot currently be cured, treatment aims to stabilise the condition, re-pigment affected areas, and help patients manage its emotional and social impact.

---

### H2: Types of Vitiligo

- **Generalised (non-segmental):** Most common. Patches appear symmetrically on both sides of the body.
- **Segmental:** Patches affect only one side or segment of the body. Less common but may respond better to treatment.
- **Focal/localised:** Small, isolated patches in one or a few locations.
- **Universal/complete:** Rare. Most of the skin surface is affected.

---

### H2: Vitiligo Treatment at Yashvini Clinic

#### H3: Topical Treatments
Topical corticosteroids and topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus) are used to attempt repigmentation of patches, particularly in early or active vitiligo. The face and smaller patches tend to respond better than areas over bony prominences.

The most widely used and effective non-surgical treatment for widespread vitiligo. Multiple sessions are required. NB-UVB works by stimulating remaining melanocytes around the hair follicles to repigment the skin.

#### H3: PUVA Therapy
Psoralen plus UVA light therapy — an older phototherapy option still used in some cases.

#### H3: Mini-Punch Grafting & Surgical Repigmentation
For stable vitiligo (no new patches for 12+ months), surgical options including mini-punch grafting or suction blister grafting can effectively transfer pigmented skin to vitiligo patches. Best results on face and neck.

### H2: Frequently Asked Questions — Vitiligo

**Q1: Is vitiligo contagious?**
No. Vitiligo is completely non-contagious. It is an autoimmune condition and cannot be spread through touch, sharing food, or any other form of contact.

**Q2: Can vitiligo be cured?**
There is currently no permanent cure for vitiligo. However, treatment can stabilise the condition and achieve meaningful repigmentation in many patients. Results are more predictable for early, localised vitiligo.

**Q3: Is vitiligo related to what I eat?**
No scientific evidence links vitiligo directly to specific foods. While some patients report personal observations, vitiligo is an autoimmune condition — not a dietary one.

**Q8: Can vitiligo be associated with other health conditions?**
Yes. Vitiligo is an autoimmune condition and may be associated with other autoimmune disorders such as thyroid disease (Hashimoto's thyroiditis, Graves' disease), type 1 diabetes, and alopecia areata. Your dermatologist may recommend screening for associated conditions.

### H2: What Are Warts?

Warts are small, rough, non-cancerous skin growths caused by infection with certain strains of the human papillomavirus (HPV). They are extremely common, particularly in children and young adults, and can appear on virtually any part of the body.

Warts are contagious — they spread through direct skin contact with the virus or indirect contact with contaminated surfaces. They are not dangerous but can be painful (particularly plantar warts), cosmetically distressing, and prone to spreading.

---

### H2: Types of Warts

- **Common warts (verruca vulgaris):** Rough, raised growths typically on hands, fingers, knees
- **Plantar warts (verruca plantaris):** On the soles of the feet; often grow inward and can be painful
- **Flat warts (verruca plana):** Smooth, flat-topped, smaller warts — often on the face, forehead, legs
- **Filiform warts:** Long, thin, thread-like projections — often around the nose, mouth, eyes
- **Periungual warts:** Around or under the fingernails and toenails
- **Mosaic warts:** Clusters of plantar warts grouped together

---

### H2: Wart Removal Treatments at Yashvini Clinic

#### H3: Cryotherapy (Liquid Nitrogen)
The most common in-office treatment. Liquid nitrogen is applied to the wart to freeze and destroy the infected tissue. The wart blisters and falls off over 1–2 weeks. Multiple sessions may be required.

#### H3: Electrocautery
Electrical current is used to burn and destroy the wart tissue. Effective for single warts, particularly filiform and small common warts. Performed under local anaesthesia.

#### H3: Chemical Treatments (Salicylic Acid, Trichloroacetic Acid)
Applied to the wart surface in clinic or as part of a home-use regimen to progressively break down wart tissue.

#### H3: Laser Treatment
CO2 laser can precisely destroy wart tissue. Used for resistant or multiple warts.

#### H3: Immunotherapy
Intralesional immunotherapy (e.g., Candida antigen injection) stimulates the immune system to fight HPV — effective for multiple or resistant warts.

Warts can recur after treatment because the HPV virus may remain in the skin. Treating warts when they are small and limiting recurrence by avoiding direct skin contact with infected surfaces is the best approach.

### H2: What Are Skin Tags?

Skin tags (acrochordons) are small, soft, benign (non-cancerous) growths of skin that hang off the skin surface on a thin stalk. Skin tags are harmless and do not require removal for medical reasons. However, they may be removed if they are cosmetically bothersome, catch on jewellery or clothing, cause irritation, or cause discomfort. They should never be attempted to be removed at home — there is a risk of bleeding, infection, and scarring.

---

### H2: Causes of Skin Tags

- Skin friction (areas where skin folds rub together)
- Obesity (more skin folds = more friction)
- Pregnancy (hormonal and weight changes)
- Type 2 diabetes (insulin resistance)
- Ageing — more common with increasing age
- Genetic predisposition
