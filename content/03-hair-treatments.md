### H1: Hair Fall Treatment in Nandyala — Diagnose the Root Cause, Restore Your Hair

### H2: What Is Hair Fall (Hair Loss)?

Hair fall — medically called alopecia — refers to the excessive loss of hair from the scalp or other body areas. It is completely normal to shed 50–100 strands of hair per day as part of the natural hair growth cycle. However, when hair shedding significantly exceeds this, noticeable thinning or bald patches develop, requiring medical attention.

Hair loss is one of the most emotionally distressing conditions we treat at Yashvini Skin & Hair Clinic. Whether you are a young woman alarmed by increasing hair on your comb, a man noticing a receding hairline, or a new mother experiencing post-pregnancy hair shedding — our approach begins with finding out why your hair is falling, not just prescribing a treatment.

---

### H2: Understanding the Hair Growth Cycle

Hair grows in a cycle with three phases:
- **Anagen (growth phase):** 2–6 years — the hair is actively growing
- **Catagen (transition phase):** 2–3 weeks — growth stops, follicle shrinks
- **Telogen (resting/shedding phase):** 2–3 months — the hair rests and then sheds

Disruptions to this cycle — from hormonal changes, nutritional deficiencies, stress, or genetics — cause various types of hair loss.

---

### H2: Common Types of Hair Loss We Treat

#### H3: Androgenetic Alopecia (Pattern Hair Loss)
The most common cause of hair loss in both men and women. In men, it presents as receding hairline and thinning at the crown. In women, it presents as diffuse thinning over the top of the scalp with preserved hairline. It is driven by genetics and androgens (male hormones).

#### H3: Telogen Effluvium
A temporary, diffuse hair shedding triggered by a shock to the hair cycle — such as post-pregnancy, major illness (including post-COVID), surgery, severe weight loss, nutritional deficiency, or prolonged stress. Usually reversible with appropriate management.

#### H3: Alopecia Areata
An autoimmune condition causing patchy hair loss on the scalp or body. Covered in detail on our Alopecia Treatment page.

#### H3: Nutritional Deficiency-Related Hair Loss
Deficiencies in iron, ferritin (iron stores), vitamin D, vitamin B12, zinc, and protein are among the most common causes of hair fall in Indian patients — particularly women.

#### H3: Hormonal Hair Loss
Thyroid disorders (hypo- and hyperthyroidism), PCOS, pregnancy, and menopause can all trigger significant hair fall.

#### H3: Scalp Conditions
Dandruff (seborrheic dermatitis), scalp psoriasis, and fungal infections of the scalp can contribute to hair fall if left untreated.

---

### H2: Causes of Hair Fall

- Genetic predisposition (androgenetic alopecia)
- Hormonal changes: pregnancy, post-partum, menopause, thyroid dysfunction, PCOS
- Nutritional deficiencies: iron, ferritin, vitamin D, B12, zinc, protein
- Excessive physical or emotional stress
- Crash dieting or rapid weight loss
- Tight hairstyles (traction alopecia — braids, tight ponytails)
- Chemical treatments (bleaching, colouring, perming — particularly repeated)
- Certain medications (chemotherapy, blood thinners, some antidepressants, oral contraceptive pills)
- Scalp infections (tinea capitis — fungal)
- Autoimmune conditions (alopecia areata, lupus)
- Post-COVID hair loss (telogen effluvium triggered by COVID-19 illness)

### H2: Frequently Asked Questions — Hair Fall Treatment

**Q1: What is the main reason for hair fall?**
Hair fall has many potential causes — the most common in Indians being nutritional deficiencies (iron, vitamin D, B12), hormonal changes (thyroid disorders, PCOS, post-pregnancy), stress, genetic pattern hair loss, and scalp conditions. A proper diagnosis is essential before starting treatment.

**Q2: How do I know if my hair fall is normal or excessive?**
Losing 50–100 strands per day is normal. If you notice clumps of hair in the shower, significantly more hair on your pillow, or visible thinning, it is time to see a dermatologist for evaluation.

**Q3: Can hair fall be stopped and reversed?**
This depends on the cause. Nutritional and telogen effluvium-related hair fall is generally reversible. Androgenetic (genetic) hair loss can be significantly slowed and hair density improved with appropriate treatment. The earlier treatment begins, the better the outcome.

**Q4: Does oil massage help hair fall?**
Oil massage can improve scalp circulation and reduce scalp dryness, which may benefit some types of hair fall. However, it will not address the underlying causes of genetic hair loss or nutritional deficiencies. It should be used as a complementary measure, not a primary treatment.

**Q5: What blood tests should I get for hair fall?**
Key blood tests include: serum ferritin (iron stores), complete blood count, thyroid function (T3/T4/TSH), vitamin D, vitamin B12, blood sugar, and (for women) hormonal profile (DHEAS, testosterone, LH, FSH). Your dermatologist will advise which tests are appropriate for you.

**Q6: Is hair fall related to stress?**
Yes. Significant physical or emotional stress can trigger telogen effluvium — a type of diffuse hair shedding that typically begins 2–3 months after the stressful event. It is usually temporary and recovers once stress levels normalise and the cause is addressed.

**Q7: What is post-COVID hair fall?**
COVID-19 illness — particularly severe cases — can trigger telogen effluvium, resulting in significant hair shedding 2–3 months after the illness. This is a temporary condition in most cases and hair typically recovers within 6–12 months with proper nutritional support and treatment.

**Q8: How long does hair fall treatment take to work?**
Reduced shedding is usually noticeable within 4–8 weeks of starting treatment. Visible improvement in hair thickness and density typically takes 3–6 months. Consistency with treatment is the most important factor.

### H1: PRP Hair Therapy in Nandyala — Stimulate Natural Hair Growth with Your Own Platelets

### H2: What Is PRP Hair Therapy?

Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) therapy for hair loss is a non-surgical, minimally invasive treatment that harnesses the healing and regenerative power of your own blood. Platelets are tiny blood cells that contain powerful growth factors — including Platelet-Derived Growth Factor (PDGF), Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF), Epidermal Growth Factor (EGF), and others — that play a key role in tissue repair and regeneration.

When injected into the scalp, PRP delivers a concentrated dose of these growth factors directly to the hair follicles. This aims to stimulate dormant follicles back into the active growth phase, increase hair shaft thickness, reduce hair shedding, and improve overall scalp health.

PRP for hair is not a cure for hair loss, but it is an evidence-supported treatment that can meaningfully improve hair density and quality, particularly when combined with other treatments for androgenetic alopecia, telogen effluvium, and alopecia areata.

---

### H2: How Is PRP Prepared?

The PRP preparation process at Yashvini Clinic follows a standardised protocol:

1. **Blood draw:** A small amount of blood (approximately 10–20ml) is drawn from the patient's arm — similar to a routine blood test.
2. **Centrifugation:** The blood is placed in a centrifuge machine that spins at high speed to separate its components. This concentrates the platelets into the plasma fraction.
3. **PRP extraction:** The platelet-rich plasma layer is extracted using sterile technique.
4. **Scalp injection:** The PRP is injected into the scalp using fine needles, targeting areas of thinning or hair loss.

The entire process takes approximately 45–60 minutes and uses only the patient's own blood — making it biocompatible with no risk of allergic reaction.

---

### H2: Who Is a Good Candidate for PRP Hair Therapy?

PRP therapy works best for:

- **Early-stage androgenetic alopecia (male and female pattern hair loss):** Particularly where follicles are miniaturised but not completely dormant
- **Telogen effluvium:** Accelerating recovery of hair after a shedding episode
- **Alopecia areata:** As part of a broader management plan
- **Patients who prefer a natural, drug-free approach** as part of their hair loss management

PRP may not be effective for:
- Advanced hair loss where follicles are completely destroyed (scarring alopecias)
- Patients on blood thinners or with platelet disorders

### H1: Alopecia Treatment in Nandyala — Expert Care for Patchy & Pattern Hair Loss

### H2: What Is Alopecia?

Alopecia is a broad medical term for hair loss. While "hair fall" is sometimes used interchangeably, alopecia specifically refers to a condition where significant hair is lost from the scalp or other areas of the body. Several distinct types of alopecia exist, each with different causes, presentations, and treatments.

The two most common types we treat at Yashvini Clinic are alopecia areata (patchy, autoimmune hair loss) and androgenetic alopecia (genetic pattern hair loss). Accurate diagnosis of the alopecia type is the essential first step — as their treatments are fundamentally different.

---

### H2: Types of Alopecia

#### H3: Alopecia Areata
An autoimmune condition in which the immune system mistakenly attacks hair follicles, causing sudden, patchy hair loss on the scalp, eyebrows, eyelashes, beard, or body. The patches are typically smooth, round, and non-scarring — meaning the follicle is not permanently damaged and hair can regrow.

- **Alopecia areata:** Patchy hair loss — one or more smooth, round patches
- **Alopecia totalis:** Complete loss of scalp hair
- **Alopecia universalis:** Complete loss of all body hair (rarest and most severe form)

#### H3: Androgenetic Alopecia (Male/Female Pattern Baldness)
The most common cause of hair loss. Driven by genetics and the effect of androgen hormones (particularly DHT — dihydrotestosterone) on susceptible hair follicles, causing them to miniaturise progressively.

- **Men:** Hairline recedes at temples; thinning at crown; follows Hamilton-Norwood classification
- **Women:** Diffuse thinning at the crown with preserved hairline; follows Ludwig classification

#### H3: Scarring Alopecias
Less common but significant — these conditions cause permanent destruction of the hair follicle through inflammation and scarring. Examples include lichen planopilaris, frontal fibrosing alopecia, and discoid lupus erythematosus. Early treatment is critical to halt progression.

#### H3: Traction Alopecia
Hair loss caused by prolonged tension on the hair — from tight braids, ponytails, buns, or hair extensions. Common at the frontal hairline and temples. Reversible in early stages.

---

### H2: Alopecia Areata Treatment at Yashvini Clinic

#### H3: Intralesional Corticosteroid Injections
The most effective treatment for patchy alopecia areata. Diluted corticosteroid is injected directly into the hairless patches, suppressing the autoimmune attack and stimulating regrowth. Sessions are repeated every 4–6 weeks.

#### H3: Topical Corticosteroids
Topical steroid solutions, foams, or ointments applied to patches — often used alongside intralesional injections or as a first-line option for children.

#### H3: Topical Minoxidil
Applied to the scalp to stimulate follicle activity and accelerate regrowth in recovering alopecia areata patches.

#### H3: Topical Immunotherapy (DPCP / SADBE)
For extensive or treatment-resistant alopecia areata. A contact sensitiser is applied to the scalp to provoke a controlled immune response that redirects the autoimmune attack away from hair follicles. Performed only in specialised centres.

#### H3: PRP Therapy
As an adjunct treatment, PRP injections may help stimulate follicular activity in alopecia areata patches.

#### H3: JAK Inhibitors (Emerging Therapies)
Newer oral medications targeting the JAK-STAT immune pathway show significant promise for severe alopecia areata. Availability and suitability are discussed during consultation.

For patchy alopecia areata, initial regrowth is typically seen within 6–12 weeks of starting intralesional injections. Full regrowth of a patch may take 3–6 months. ### H2: Frequently Asked Questions — Alopecia Treatment

**Q1: What is alopecia areata and what causes it?**
Alopecia areata is an autoimmune condition where the immune system attacks hair follicles. The exact trigger is unknown, but genetic predisposition and immune dysregulation are involved. Stress can worsen or trigger flares.

**Q2: Will hair grow back after alopecia areata?**
In most cases of patchy alopecia areata, hair can regrow — either spontaneously or with treatment. The prognosis is less predictable for alopecia totalis and universalis. Follicles are not permanently damaged in alopecia areata, which is why regrowth is possible.

**Q3: Is alopecia areata contagious?**
No. Alopecia areata is an autoimmune condition — it cannot be spread from person to person.

**Q4: Can children get alopecia areata?**
Yes. Alopecia areata affects children and adults. It is one of the more common causes of hair loss in children. Treatment is adapted appropriately for paediatric patients.

**Q5: What is the difference between alopecia areata and androgenetic alopecia?**
Alopecia areata causes sudden, patchy hair loss due to autoimmunity. Androgenetic alopecia (pattern baldness) is gradual, driven by genetics and hormones, and follows a predictable pattern. They have different treatments.

**Q6: Is alopecia related to stress?**
Stress is commonly reported as a trigger for alopecia areata flares. However, the underlying condition is autoimmune — stress can worsen it but is not the sole cause.

**Q7: Can alopecia areata be permanently cured?**
There is no permanent cure for alopecia areata. However, effective treatment can achieve regrowth in most patchy cases. The condition may recur and requires monitoring and ongoing management in some patients.

**Q8: Are there any new treatments for severe alopecia?**
Yes. JAK inhibitors (such as baricitinib and ruxolitinib) are a major advance in treatment for severe alopecia areata and totalis. Discuss availability and suitability with your dermatologist.

### H1: Dandruff Treatment in Nandyala — Clear Your Scalp, Boost Your Confidence

### H2: What Is Dandruff?

Dandruff — medically known as pityriasis capitis or seborrheic dermatitis of the scalp — is a very common, chronic condition characterised by flaking of the scalp skin, often accompanied by itching. The white or yellowish flakes shed from the scalp onto hair, shoulders, and clothing, causing social embarrassment for many.

Dandruff is not caused by poor hygiene (though hygiene matters in management). It is driven primarily by a combination of excess oil production (seborrhoea), the presence of Malassezia yeast (a naturally occurring fungus on all scalps), and individual immune sensitivity to this yeast.

When dandruff involves significant redness, greasy scaling, and extends to the face, eyebrows, ears, or chest — it is better classified as seborrheic dermatitis, a more widespread form of the same condition.

---

### H2: Causes of Dandruff

- **Malassezia yeast overgrowth:** The primary driver — Malassezia breaks down scalp oils into irritating fatty acids, triggering increased cell turnover and flaking
- **Excess sebum (oil) production:** Creates a favourable environment for yeast
- **Dry scalp:** Dry winter air or over-washing can cause dry scalp flaking that mimics dandruff
- **Sensitivity to hair products:** Certain shampoos, conditioners, or styling products
- **Infrequent hair washing:** Allows oil and dead skin to accumulate
- **Stress:** Known to worsen dandruff
- **Hormonal changes**
- **Nutritional deficiencies:** Zinc and B-vitamin deficiencies have been linked to dandruff

---

### H2: Symptoms

- White or yellowish flakes on scalp, hair, shoulders
- Itchy scalp — often worse in winter or dry seasons
- Greasy scalp patches with yellowish scaling (seborrheic dermatitis)
- Redness of the scalp in more severe cases
- Extension to eyebrows, sides of nose, ears, or chest (seborrheic dermatitis)

### H2: Frequently Asked Questions — Dandruff Treatment

**Q1: What causes dandruff and how do I get rid of it?**
Dandruff is primarily caused by Malassezia yeast overgrowth on an oily scalp. It is best treated with medicated antifungal shampoos (ketoconazole, zinc pyrithione, selenium sulphide) and appropriate scalp care. A dermatologist can prescribe the most effective formulation for your scalp type.

**Q2: Is dandruff a sign of poor hygiene?**
Not necessarily. Dandruff is caused by a yeast (Malassezia), hormonal factors, and individual scalp sensitivity — not poor hygiene. However, infrequent washing allows oil and skin cells to accumulate, which can worsen dandruff.

**Q3: Can dandruff cause hair fall?**
Severe dandruff and the associated scalp inflammation and itching can contribute to hair fall through two mechanisms: mechanical shedding from scratching and inflammation around the follicle. Treating dandruff effectively can help reduce associated hair fall.

**Q4: What is the best shampoo for dandruff?**
The best shampoo depends on your dandruff type. For most cases, ketoconazole 2% shampoo is the most effective antifungal option. Zinc pyrithione and selenium sulphide are effective for maintenance. Your dermatologist will recommend the most appropriate option.

**Q5: Is dandruff the same as dry scalp?**
Not exactly. Dry scalp causes fine, white flakes with minimal oiliness. Dandruff (seborrheic dermatitis) causes larger, often yellowish, greasier flakes with scalp oiliness. The treatment approach differs slightly.

**Q6: Can dandruff be cured permanently?**
Dandruff is a chronic condition and tends to recur. It can be very effectively controlled with regular medicated shampoo use and proper scalp care. Most patients achieve excellent control with ongoing maintenance treatment.

**Q7: Why does my dandruff get worse in winter?**
Cold, dry air strips the scalp of moisture, increasing flaking. The immune system also behaves differently in colder months. Switching to a more moisturising antifungal shampoo in winter and avoiding hot water during washing can help.

**Q8: Can I use coconut oil for dandruff?**
Coconut oil has some antifungal properties, but it is also an oil — which can feed Malassezia yeast if left on the scalp. Heavy oiling of the scalp is generally not recommended for dandruff-prone individuals. A light application massaged in and washed off promptly is less problematic than overnight oiling.

### H1: Telogen Effluvium Treatment in Nandyala — Understanding & Recovering from Sudden Hair Shedding

### H2: What Is Telogen Effluvium?

Telogen effluvium (TE) is a common and highly distressing form of hair loss characterised by diffuse, excessive shedding of hair from all over the scalp. Unlike gradual pattern hair loss, telogen effluvium typically begins suddenly — often alarming patients with dramatic clumps of hair on combs, pillows, and in the shower.

It occurs when a significant physiological or psychological "shock" pushes large numbers of hair follicles simultaneously out of the active growth (anagen) phase and into the resting (telogen) phase prematurely. This results in widespread simultaneous shedding approximately 2–3 months after the triggering event — which is why patients often struggle to identify what caused it.

The good news: Telogen effluvium is almost always temporary and reversible when the underlying trigger is identified and addressed.

---

### H2: Common Triggers of Telogen Effluvium

- **Post-pregnancy (postpartum telogen effluvium):** One of the most common causes — oestrogen levels drop sharply after delivery, triggering mass hair shedding typically 2–4 months post-partum
- **Major illness or fever:** Including post-COVID-19 hair loss (very common)
- **Surgery or hospitalisation**
- **Severe emotional stress or trauma**
- **Significant nutritional deficiency:** Iron/ferritin deficiency is the most common — also vitamin D, B12, zinc, protein
- **Crash dieting or rapid weight loss**
- **Thyroid disorder:** Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism can trigger TE
- **Starting or stopping hormonal medications:** Including oral contraceptive pills
- **Chronic illness**
- **Anaemia**

---

### H2: Symptoms of Telogen Effluvium

- Sudden, diffuse hair shedding across the entire scalp (not patchy)
- Significantly more hair than usual on the comb, brush, pillow, and shower floor
- The scalp typically remains normal in appearance (no scaling, redness, or patchiness)
- Hair density may visibly reduce across the whole scalp
- Symptoms begin 2–3 months after the triggering event

---

Diagnosis involves:

2. Hair pull test — a clinical test where small groups of hair are gently pulled to assess the number of telogen hairs
3. Trichoscopy — scalp and hair follicle analysis
4. Blood tests: Serum ferritin, CBC, thyroid profile, vitamin D, B12, blood sugar, hormonal profile as relevant

### H2: Frequently Asked Questions — Telogen Effluvium

**Q1: What is telogen effluvium and why does it cause so much hair fall?**
Telogen effluvium occurs when a physical or emotional shock triggers large numbers of hair follicles to prematurely enter the resting (telogen) phase. When these hairs shed simultaneously 2–3 months later, the result is alarming widespread hair loss.

**Q2: Will my hair grow back after telogen effluvium?**
In the vast majority of cases, yes — telogen effluvium is a temporary, reversible condition. Once the trigger is addressed, hair follicles return to their growth cycle and hair density recovers, typically within 6–12 months.

**Q3: How much hair loss is normal in telogen effluvium?**
Patients with TE can shed several times the normal daily hair count — sometimes hundreds of hairs per day at peak shedding. While alarming, this typically resolves as the condition runs its course.

**Q4: Can stress cause hair fall after a few months?**
Yes. Significant stress can trigger telogen effluvium, with hair shedding beginning approximately 2–3 months after the stressful event. This delay often makes it difficult for patients to connect the hair loss to its cause.

**Q5: Is post-pregnancy hair fall normal?**
Yes. Postpartum telogen effluvium — hair shedding after delivery — is very common and affects many women. It is triggered by the drop in oestrogen levels after birth. It is temporary and hair typically recovers within 6–12 months.

**Q6: Can COVID cause hair fall?**
Yes. Post-COVID telogen effluvium is a well-documented phenomenon, typically occurring 2–3 months after the illness. Recovery follows the natural course of telogen effluvium — usually within 6–12 months with appropriate nutritional support.

**Q7: What is the difference between telogen effluvium and androgenetic alopecia?**
Telogen effluvium causes sudden, diffuse shedding triggered by a specific event — it is temporary and reversible. Androgenetic alopecia (pattern hair loss) is a gradual, progressive thinning driven by genetics and hormones, requiring long-term management.

**Q8: Is iron deficiency related to hair fall?**
Yes. Iron (particularly ferritin — stored iron) deficiency is one of the most common triggers of telogen effluvium in Indian women. Even when anaemia is not present, low ferritin can significantly impact hair growth. Testing ferritin — not just haemoglobin — is essential.
