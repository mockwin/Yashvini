### H1: Skin Care Tips for Healthy Indian Skin — Expert Advice from Yashvini Clinic

### H2: Why Skin Care Matters — Especially in India

India's tropical climate, high UV index, pollution levels, and the unique characteristics of South Asian skin types make a consistent, appropriate skincare routine not just a cosmetic luxury — but a genuine health necessity. Proper skin care can prevent premature ageing, manage common conditions like acne and pigmentation, and protect against long-term sun damage.

At Yashvini Skin & Hair Clinic, we believe that the best skincare advice is simple, practical, evidence-based, and adapted to your specific skin type and lifestyle.

---

### H2: Know Your Skin Type

Before anything else, understanding your skin type helps you choose the right products and routine:

- **Oily skin:** Excess sebum, shiny appearance, prone to acne and enlarged pores. Needs oil-free, non-comedogenic products.
- **Dry skin:** Feels tight, may be flaky, prone to sensitivity. Needs rich moisturisers and gentle cleansers.
- **Combination skin:** Oily in the T-zone (forehead, nose, chin), normal or dry on the cheeks. Needs balanced, lightweight products.
- **Sensitive skin:** Reacts easily to products, prone to redness and irritation. Needs fragrance-free, minimal-ingredient products.
- **Normal skin:** Well-balanced, not too oily or too dry. Standard routine works well.

**Tip:** Indian skin, particularly South Indian skin, is commonly oily to combination with tendency towards pigmentation and sun sensitivity. Your routine should account for these characteristics.

---

### H2: The Essential Daily Skincare Routine

#### H3: Morning Routine

**Step 1: Cleanse**
Use a gentle, sulphate-free cleanser suited to your skin type. Wash your face with lukewarm (not hot) water. Avoid over-washing — once in the morning and once at night is sufficient for most skin types.

**Step 2: Vitamin C Serum (optional but highly recommended)**
A vitamin C serum applied in the morning provides antioxidant protection against UV-induced damage, brightens skin, and supports collagen. Choose a stable formulation (L-ascorbic acid or ascorbyl glucoside).

**Step 3: Moisturise**
Even oily skin needs moisturisation. Skipping it can actually increase oil production. Choose a lightweight, oil-free gel or lotion moisturiser for oily skin; a richer cream for dry skin.

**Step 4: Sunscreen — Non-Negotiable**
Apply a broad-spectrum SPF 50+ sunscreen every single morning — even on cloudy days, even indoors (if near windows). UV passes through clouds and glass. Reapply every 2–3 hours if outdoors. This is the single most important step for preventing pigmentation, premature ageing, and protecting skin health.

#### H3: Evening Routine

**Step 1: Double Cleanse (if wearing makeup or sunscreen)**
Use a gentle oil cleanser or micellar water to remove sunscreen and makeup, followed by your regular gentle cleanser.

**Step 2: Treatment Products (if prescribed)**
Apply any prescribed medications (retinoids, hydroquinone, benzoyl peroxide) as directed by your dermatologist. These are typically used at night.

**Step 3: Moisturise**
Apply your appropriate moisturiser. A richer cream can be used at night as it does not need to sit under sunscreen.

---

### H2: Sunscreen — The Most Important Skincare Step

Sunscreen deserves its own section because it is that important, particularly in India:

- **SPF 30 is the minimum; SPF 50+ is recommended** for Indian climate and skin type
- **Broad-spectrum** means it protects against both UVA (ageing rays) and UVB (burning rays)
- **Amount matters:** Use approximately ¼ teaspoon (1.25ml) for the face alone
- **Reapplication is essential:** Every 2–3 hours when outdoors, regardless of SPF number
- **No sunscreen is waterproof forever:** Reapply after swimming or heavy sweating
- **Sunscreen and vitamin C together** in the morning offer powerful combined protection

---

### H2: Common Skincare Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not using sunscreen daily** — the most common and costly mistake
2. **Using too many products at once** — less is more; too many active ingredients cause irritation
3. **Picking acne** — leads to scarring and spreading of bacteria
4. **Using steroid-containing "fairness creams"** — cause long-term skin damage, thinning, and worse pigmentation
5. **Washing face too frequently** — strips natural oils, increases oil production in response
6. **Skipping moisturiser for oily skin** — causes more oil production and dehydrated skin
7. **Applying products in the wrong order** — apply from thinnest to thickest consistency
8. **Not doing a patch test for new products** — particularly important for sensitive skin
9. **Expecting overnight results** — most skincare treatments take 4–8 weeks to show visible improvement
10. **Using expired products** — inactive or degraded ingredients at best; irritating at worst

---

### H2: Nutrition & Hydration for Skin Health

**Hydration:** Drink adequate water daily (8–10 glasses in India's climate). Dehydration shows directly on the skin as dullness and emphasised fine lines.

**Antioxidant-rich diet:** Fruits, vegetables, nuts, and seeds provide vitamins C and E, zinc, and polyphenols that support skin health from within.

**Protein:** Hair and skin are built from protein. Include adequate protein in your diet (dal, eggs, meat, fish, legumes).

**Omega-3 fatty acids:** Found in fatty fish, flaxseed, and walnuts — support skin barrier function and reduce inflammation.

**Limit high glycaemic foods:** White rice, white bread, sugary drinks — associated with worsening acne and accelerated skin ageing in some individuals.

### H2: Skincare Tips for Specific Skin Concerns

**For Acne-Prone Skin:**
- Use non-comedogenic, oil-free products
- Cleanse after sweating
- Avoid touching your face
- Change pillowcases every 2–3 days
- Never pop pimples

**For Pigmentation-Prone Skin:**
- Sunscreen is your primary treatment tool
- Use a vitamin C serum in the morning
- Introduce gentle exfoliation (AHA/BHA) gradually and carefully

**For Dry, Sensitive Skin:**
- Avoid hot water for washing
- Choose fragrance-free, minimal-ingredient products
- Apply moisturiser while skin is still slightly damp
- Avoid strong actives without dermatologist guidance

---

### H2: When to See a Dermatologist

Self-care has limits. See a dermatologist if you:
- Have persistent acne that does not respond to over-the-counter products
- Notice new or changing moles or skin lesions
- Have a widespread, unexplained rash
- Experience hair fall beyond normal levels
- Have a skin condition that is affecting your quality of life or confidence

### H1: Hair Care Tips for Healthy Indian Hair — Expert Guidance from Yashvini Clinic

### H2: Understanding Indian Hair

Indian hair tends to be naturally dark, thick, and coarse — characteristics that come with both strengths and vulnerabilities. South Indian hair in particular is often wavy to curly in texture, requiring specific care approaches that differ from fine, straight hair types common in other parts of the world.

The most common hair concerns among our patients include hair fall, hair thinning, dandruff, dry and frizzy hair, and damage from chemical or heat treatments. Good hair care, combined with proper nutrition and — when needed — medical treatment, can significantly improve hair health.

---

### H2: The Basics of Healthy Hair Care

#### H3: How Often Should You Wash Your Hair?

There is no single right answer — it depends on your scalp type:

- **Oily scalp:** Wash every 2–3 days to prevent oil and yeast build-up (which contributes to dandruff and hair fall)
- **Normal scalp:** Every 3–4 days works for most people
- **Dry scalp:** Every 5–7 days; over-washing strips natural oils further

Use lukewarm (not hot) water. Hot water strips scalp oils and causes hair shaft damage.

#### H3: Choosing the Right Shampoo

- Choose a shampoo suited to your scalp condition (oily, dry, dandruff-prone)
- Avoid sulfate-heavy shampoos for dry or chemically treated hair
- If you have dandruff, use a medicated antifungal shampoo as prescribed
- Clarifying shampoos (used once a month) help remove product build-up

#### H3: Conditioning — What to Know

- Apply conditioner to the lengths and ends of hair — not to the scalp
- Applying conditioner to the scalp can weigh hair down and worsen oiliness
- For dry or chemically treated hair, a weekly deep conditioning mask helps restore moisture and strength

---

### H2: Hair Oiling — What the Evidence Says

Hair oiling is a deeply ingrained practice in South Indian culture — and some aspects of it are genuinely beneficial. However, it is also widely misunderstood:

**Benefits of hair oiling (moderate, appropriate use):**
- Reduces combing-related breakage (oil lubricates the hair shaft)
- Scalp massage improves blood circulation
- Coconut oil can penetrate the hair shaft and reduce protein loss in undamaged hair
- A light pre-wash oil application helps reduce the "roughening" effect of shampoo on the hair cuticle

**What oiling does NOT do:**
- It does not treat pattern hair loss or nutritional hair fall at the root level
- Leaving oil on the scalp overnight feeds Malassezia yeast and can worsen dandruff
- Very heavy oiling can clog follicle openings if not thoroughly washed out

**Recommended approach:**
Apply a moderate amount of oil to the lengths and scalp 1–2 hours before washing. Massage gently. Wash thoroughly. Avoid overnight oiling if you have dandruff.

---

### H2: Diet for Healthy Hair — Nutrition Matters

Hair is made primarily of keratin — a protein. The following nutrients are essential for healthy hair growth:

| Nutrient | Role in Hair Health | Food Sources |
|---|---|---|
| **Protein** | Building block of hair (keratin) | Eggs, dal, paneer, meat, fish, legumes |
| **Iron/Ferritin** | Delivers oxygen to follicles | Spinach, beetroot, meat, legumes |
| **Vitamin D** | Stimulates hair follicles | Sunlight, eggs, fortified milk, fatty fish |
| **Vitamin B12** | Essential for red blood cells & hair growth | Meat, dairy, eggs; supplementation for vegetarians |
| **Zinc** | Tissue growth and repair | Nuts, seeds, whole grains, meat |
| **Biotin** | Keratin production | Eggs, nuts, sweet potato |
| **Omega-3 fatty acids** | Scalp health, reduces inflammation | Flaxseed, walnuts, fatty fish |

A vegetarian diet can be entirely compatible with healthy hair — but requires conscious attention to iron, B12, and zinc intake. If hair fall persists despite good diet, a blood test to check ferritin, B12, thyroid, and vitamin D levels is strongly recommended.

---

### H2: Protecting Hair from Damage

#### H3: Heat Styling
- Use heat protectant spray before applying any heat styling tool
- Keep flat irons and curling irons at the lowest effective temperature
- Air-dry hair when possible — heat styling every day causes cumulative damage

#### H3: Chemical Treatments
- Space out colouring, bleaching, and perming sessions — ideally no more than once every 6–8 weeks minimum
- Bleaching (especially for grey coverage) significantly damages hair structure — use bond-strengthening treatments alongside
- Go for a professional trim every 6–8 weeks to remove split ends — preventing further damage travelling up the shaft

#### H3: Tight Hairstyles
- Avoid chronically tight ponytails, braids, and buns — these cause traction alopecia (hair loss at the hairline)
- Use scrunchies or soft hair ties rather than tight rubber bands

---

### H2: When to See a Dermatologist for Hair Problems

- More than 100 strands falling per day for more than 4 weeks
- Visible thinning at the crown or widening parting
- Patchy bald spots (sudden or gradual)
- Scalp that is persistently itchy, flaky, or red
- Hair that has become significantly finer, weaker, or more brittle
- Hair fall after illness, surgery, or significant stress

### H1: Seasonal Skin Care in India — How to Adapt Your Routine All Year Round

### H2: Why Seasons Matter for Your Skin

India's climate varies dramatically across regions and seasons — from scorching 45°C summer heat to humid monsoons, cool winter months, and dry transitional seasons. Each season brings distinct challenges for skin and hair. The key to year-round skin health is adapting your routine to meet these seasonal demands.

---

### H2: Summer Skin Care (March–June)

India's summers are intense, with high temperatures, extreme UV radiation, and heavy sweating — particularly in South India. This is the most demanding season for skin.

#### H3: Key Summer Challenges
- Intense UV exposure causing sunburn, tanning, and pigmentation
- Excess sweating leading to clogged pores and acne breakouts
- Increased oil production worsening acne and dandruff
- Heat-induced rashes (prickly heat / miliaria)
- Dehydration affecting skin plumpness and radiance

#### H3: Summer Skin Care Tips
**Sunscreen:** SPF 50+ broad-spectrum sunscreen is absolutely essential. Apply 20 minutes before sun exposure and reapply every 2 hours when outdoors. Carry a travel-sized sunscreen for reapplication.

**Lightweight products:** Switch to gel-based, oil-free moisturisers and serums during summer. Heavy creams feel uncomfortable and may clog pores in heat and humidity.

**Gentle cleansing:** Wash your face twice daily — morning and evening — but no more. Use a gentle foaming cleanser for oily/combination skin.

**Stay hydrated:** Drink at least 8–10 glasses of water daily. Coconut water, buttermilk, and fresh fruit juices help with electrolyte replenishment.

**Antioxidants:** A vitamin C serum in the morning (under sunscreen) provides added protection against UV-generated free radicals.

**Minimal makeup:** Heavy makeup in summer clogs pores and contributes to breakouts. Opt for tinted moisturisers with SPF or lightweight BB creams if needed.

**Prickly heat:** Keep skin cool and dry. Wear loose, breathable cotton clothing. Calamine lotion can soothe prickly heat. If persistent or severe, see a dermatologist.

**Sun-protective habits:** Seek shade during peak UV hours (10am–4pm). Wear a wide-brimmed hat, UV-protective sunglasses, and carry an umbrella.

---

### H2: Monsoon Skin Care (July–September)

The monsoon brings relief from summer heat but creates its own skin challenges — high humidity, increased fungal infections, and skin congestion.

#### H3: Key Monsoon Challenges
- High humidity increases oil production and causes skin congestion
- Fungal infections (tinea, candida) thrive in moist, warm conditions
- Acne breakouts worsen with humidity and sweating
- Dandruff tends to flare
- Insect bites are more common

#### H3: Monsoon Skin Care Tips
**Cleansing:** Keep skin clean — wash face twice daily. Increased humidity means more sweat and oil, so cleansing is important. However, avoid over-washing which strips the skin.

**Antifungal awareness:** Keep skin folds (groin, underarms, under breasts, between toes) clean and dry. Change into dry clothes promptly after getting wet. Use an antifungal powder if prone to fungal infections in these areas.

**Hydration:** Despite the humidity, drink adequate water — outdoor humidity does not hydrate your skin from within.

**Sunscreen — still important:** UV rays are not blocked by cloud cover. Continue SPF 50+ sunscreen daily.

**Lighter moisturisers:** Continue with lightweight, gel-based moisturisers. Heavy creams in humid weather can lead to congestion and breakouts.

**Foot care:** Keep feet dry. Wear open or moisture-wicking footwear where possible. Dry between toes thoroughly after getting wet — a prime site for athlete's foot in monsoon.

**Hair care in monsoon:** The humidity can cause frizz and scalp oiliness. Avoid leaving hair wet for long periods — damp scalps encourage dandruff and fungal growth. Shampoo regularly.

---

### H2: Winter Skin Care (November–February)

In much of South India, winter is mild — but even the relative drop in temperature and humidity can significantly affect skin hydration and barrier function. In North India, winters are genuinely harsh.

#### H3: Key Winter Challenges
- Reduced humidity causes dry, tight, flaky skin
- Skin barrier weakens, increasing sensitivity
- Eczema and psoriasis tend to flare
- Lips become chapped
- Hot shower temptation — hot water is damaging to skin despite feeling good

#### H3: Winter Skin Care Tips
**Switch to richer moisturisers:** Upgrade from lightweight summer gels to cream-based moisturisers. Apply moisturiser immediately after bathing, while skin is still slightly damp, to seal in moisture.

**Avoid hot showers:** Hot water strips the skin's natural oils. Use lukewarm water and limit shower time.

**Gentle cleansers:** Switch to creamy, non-stripping cleansers. Avoid harsh foaming cleansers in winter.

**Lips:** Apply a medicated lip balm (petroleum jelly or one with shea butter and SPF) daily. Avoid licking lips — saliva actually dries them out further.

**Sunscreen — still necessary:** Winter sun (especially morning sun) still causes UV damage. SPF 30+ minimum, SPF 50 preferred.

**Eczema management:** If you have eczema, winter is the highest risk season for flare-ups. Increase moisturisation frequency, use prescribed medications, and use a room humidifier if possible.

**Hands and feet:** These tend to get the driest in winter. Apply a rich hand cream after every hand wash.

---

### H2: Transition Season Skin Care (October and February–March)

The transitional seasons — as India moves between summer and winter or winter and summer — can cause the skin to become confused and unpredictable.

#### H3: Key Transition Season Challenges
- Skin may fluctuate between being oily (residual summer behaviour) and dry (adjusting to cooler temperatures)
- Sudden temperature changes can trigger sensitivity and rosacea flares
- Allergy season — pollen and dust trigger skin allergies in susceptible individuals

#### H3: Transition Season Tips
**Gradual routine change:** Do not dramatically change your entire skincare routine overnight. Gradually introduce richer moisturisers as temperatures drop, or lighter ones as heat increases.

**Continue sunscreen:** Year-round sun protection is non-negotiable regardless of season.

**Watch for allergies:** If you develop unexplained skin rashes or hives during transition seasons, seasonal allergens may be the trigger. Consult a dermatologist.

**Reassess and adjust:** Transition seasons are a good time to review your skincare routine with your dermatologist and adjust for the upcoming season.

### H1: 15 Skin Care Myths Debunked by Our Dermatologist

*Every day our patients come in having tried treatments based on advice from well-meaning friends, family members, social media influencers, or internet forums. Many of these popular beliefs are harmless — but some can genuinely damage your skin. Let us set the record straight.*

---

#### Myth 1: "You only need sunscreen when it's sunny"

**FACT:** UV radiation penetrates cloud cover. On an overcast day, you still receive 80% of the UV exposure you would on a clear day. Additionally, UVA rays — responsible for premature ageing and pigmentation — pass through glass windows. SPF 50+ sunscreen should be applied every morning, rain or shine, indoors or outdoors.

---

#### Myth 2: "Oily skin doesn't need moisturiser"

**FACT:** Moisturiser and oil are not the same thing. Oily skin can be dehydrated — lacking water content despite having excess oil. Skipping moisturiser actually signals the skin to produce more oil in compensation. Use a lightweight, oil-free, non-comedogenic moisturiser — it keeps your skin balanced.

---

#### Myth 3: "Fairness creams will permanently lighten my skin"

**FACT:** Most over-the-counter fairness creams rely on ingredients that either provide a temporary whitening effect or, more dangerously, contain undisclosed steroids. Steroid-containing creams initially lighten skin but cause significant long-term damage — skin thinning, acne, stretch marks, and severe rebound darkening when stopped. Always seek medically formulated treatment for pigmentation.

---

#### Myth 4: "Washing your face more frequently clears acne"

**FACT:** Over-washing strips the skin's natural protective barrier, triggering excess oil production — which can worsen acne. Washing twice daily (morning and evening) with a gentle, appropriate cleanser is sufficient. More is not better.

---

#### Myth 5: "Toothpaste clears pimples overnight"

**FACT:** Toothpaste contains ingredients that can irritate, dry, and chemically burn the skin. It is not formulated for skin use and can cause redness, peeling, and post-inflammatory dark marks. Use a targeted acne spot treatment containing benzoyl peroxide or salicylic acid instead.

---

#### Myth 6: "Natural ingredients are always safe for the skin"

**FACT:** Many natural substances are potent skin irritants. Lemon juice (highly acidic, can cause photosensitivity and burns), neat essential oils, turmeric masks, and raw egg white can all damage skin or trigger allergic reactions. "Natural" does not automatically mean safe or suitable for skin use.

---

#### Myth 7: "Drinking a lot of water will make your skin glow"

**FACT:** Staying properly hydrated supports overall health and prevents your skin from becoming severely dehydrated. However, drinking water beyond adequate levels does not "flush" skin clear or produce dramatic glow. Skin radiance is driven by skincare, nutrition, sun protection, and overall health — not water volume alone.

---

#### Myth 8: "Anti-ageing creams will completely remove wrinkles"

**FACT:** No over-the-counter cream can "remove" wrinkles. Proven active ingredients like retinoids (prescription-grade) can improve fine lines significantly over months of consistent use. Most OTC "anti-ageing" creams provide hydration that temporarily plumps skin. For meaningful wrinkle reduction, dermatologist-supervised treatments are needed.

---

#### Myth 9: "Acne only affects teenagers"

**FACT:** Acne affects people of all ages. Adult acne — particularly in women — is increasingly recognised and is often driven by hormonal factors (PCOS, menstrual cycle fluctuations), stress, and certain medications. It responds well to appropriate dermatological treatment.

---

#### Myth 10: "Higher SPF means you can spend more time in the sun"

**FACT:** SPF number indicates the level of protection from UVB — not the amount of time you can stay in the sun. SPF 50 blocks approximately 98% of UVB; SPF 30 blocks approximately 97%. The key is reapplication — every 2 hours — regardless of SPF number. SPF 50 does not grant permission to skip reapplication.

---

#### Myth 11: "Coconut oil is a good moisturiser for all skin types"

**FACT:** Coconut oil works reasonably well for dry skin on the body but is comedogenic (pore-clogging) for many people's facial skin. It can worsen acne on acne-prone faces. It is not universally suitable as a facial moisturiser.

---

#### Myth 12: "Steroid creams are a quick fix for all skin problems"

**FACT:** Steroid creams are effective for specific conditions when used appropriately and for limited durations under medical supervision. Misuse — particularly applying them to fungal infections, acne, or for generalised "fairness" — causes serious complications including skin thinning, stretch marks, bacterial and fungal infections, and facial hair growth. Never apply steroid-containing products to the face without a doctor's prescription.

---

#### Myth 13: "Once acne scars form, nothing can be done about them"

**FACT:** Acne scars can be significantly improved with modern dermatological treatments including microneedling, chemical peels, laser resurfacing, subcision, and dermal fillers. While complete elimination of deep structural scars is not always possible, a 50–80% improvement is commonly achievable.

---

#### Myth 14: "Hair loss is only a man's problem"

**FACT:** Hair loss affects women significantly — and often more emotionally than men. Common causes in women include nutritional deficiencies (iron/ferritin), thyroid disorders, PCOS, postpartum hormonal changes, and androgenetic alopecia. Women's hair loss is extremely common, very treatable, and should not be dismissed or normalised.

---

#### Myth 15: "Expensive skincare products always work better"

**FACT:** Price does not necessarily correlate with efficacy. Some of the most evidence-backed skincare ingredients — vitamin C, niacinamide, retinoids, hyaluronic acid, and mineral sunscreen — are available in very reasonably priced formulations. The most expensive product at a pharmacy is not automatically the best for your skin. A dermatologist can guide you to the most effective products for your budget.

### H1: Pre & Post Treatment Care at Yashvini Skin & Hair Clinic — Your Complete Guide

### H2: Why Pre and Post Care Matters

The results of any dermatological procedure depend not only on the skill of the practitioner and the quality of the equipment — they depend equally on what you do before and after the treatment. Proper pre-care prepares your skin to respond optimally. Proper post-care protects the results and prevents complications.

At Yashvini Clinic, we always provide written pre and post-care instructions specific to your treatment. This page provides a general overview.

---

### H2: General Pre-Treatment Care (Applies to Most Procedures)

**2–4 Weeks Before (for peels, laser, microneedling):**
- Begin using prescribed pre-treatment topicals if recommended (often includes retinoid taper, hydroquinone, or vitamin C)
- Start daily SPF 50+ sunscreen application if you are not already
- Avoid prolonged sun exposure — tan skin significantly increases risk of PIH after procedures

**1 Week Before:**
- Discontinue use of active skincare ingredients (strong retinoids, glycolic acid, vitamin C, AHAs/BHAs) unless directed otherwise
- Avoid waxing, threading, or bleaching the treatment area
- Do not take blood-thinning supplements (fish oil, vitamin E, aspirin/NSAIDs) for PRP sessions — discuss with your doctor

**Day Before / Day of Procedure:**
- Arrive with clean skin — no makeup, sunscreen, or heavy products (unless instructed otherwise)
- Eat a light meal before any injectable procedure (PRP, intralesional injections)
- Inform the doctor of any changes in health, new medications, or recent sun exposure

---

### H2: Post-Treatment Care by Procedure

#### H3: After Chemical Peels

**First 24 hours:**
- Apply prescribed soothing cream gently; avoid touching the face unnecessarily
- Apply SPF 50+ sunscreen before leaving the clinic (your doctor will apply it)
- Avoid makeup entirely

**Days 1–7:**
- Use only the prescribed/gentle cleanser and moisturiser
- Apply sunscreen every morning; reapply if going outdoors
- DO NOT pick, scratch, or peel any flaking skin — let it shed naturally
- Avoid heat (saunas, steam, hot showers), exercise, and swimming for 5–7 days
- Avoid all active skincare ingredients (retinoids, AHAs, vitamin C) until peeling is complete

**When to call us:**
- Excessive blistering, weeping, or pain beyond normal
- Unusual darkening of skin (paradoxical PIH — though this can sometimes occur temporarily)

---

#### H3: After Microneedling

**First 24 hours:**
- Skin will appear red, similar to a sunburn — this is normal
- Apply prescribed soothing serum (hyaluronic acid) gently with clean hands
- Sleep on a clean pillowcase; avoid touching the face unnecessarily
- Avoid makeup, active ingredients, and retinoids

**Days 2–5:**
- Mild dryness, flaking, and roughness is normal
- Continue gentle cleanser + soothing serum + SPF 50+ only
- Avoid sun exposure, saunas, gym, and swimming

**After Day 5:**
- Gradually reintroduce your normal skincare over 5–7 days
- Results will continue to develop over the following 4–6 weeks

---

#### H3: After Laser Hair Removal

**First 24 hours:**
- Apply cool gel or ice pack (wrapped in cloth) to soothe the treated area
- Do not apply any deodorant or fragrance products to treated underarms
- Avoid tight clothing over treated areas

**First Week:**
- Strict sun avoidance on treated areas — apply SPF 50+ if exposure unavoidable
- Avoid waxing, threading (shaving is permitted)
- Avoid hot baths, saunas, steam, swimming pools for 48–72 hours
- Avoid self-tanning products throughout the course

**Hair shedding:**
- Treated hairs will appear to "grow" over 1–3 weeks post-session — this is the hair being expelled, not new growth. Do not wax — shave or leave it alone.

---

#### H3: After PRP (Hair or Skin)

**First 24 hours:**
- Do not wash hair or touch the scalp (for hair PRP)
- Avoid alcohol for 24 hours
- Mild scalp swelling or redness is normal

**Days 2–5:**
- Resume gentle hair washing and normal scalp routine
- Avoid vigorous exercise for 24–48 hours
- Continue prescribed minoxidil or other hair medications

**Ongoing:**
- Maintain scheduled follow-up sessions for the full PRP course
- Continue nutritional supplements as prescribed

---

#### H3: After Wart/Skin Tag Removal

**Wound care:**
- Keep the area clean and dry
- Apply prescribed antiseptic or wound cream as directed
- Cover with a clean dressing if recommended

**Healing:**
- A small scab will form at the site and shed naturally within 1–2 weeks
- Do not pick the scab — this increases scarring risk
- Protect the area from sun exposure during healing

**When to call us:**
- Signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, yellow/green discharge, fever

---

### H2: General Post-Treatment Principles (All Procedures)

1. **Sunscreen every day** — non-negotiable. This protects your results and prevents PIH.
2. **Gentle cleansing and moisturising** — the skin is recovering; do not stress it further
3. **No active ingredients unless prescribed** — wait for your doctor's go-ahead
4. **No picking or scratching** — let healing happen naturally
5. **Stay hydrated** — drink water and nourish skin from within
6. **Follow up as scheduled** — adjustments to treatment may be needed based on your response
7. **Report anything unusual** — do not wait for your next appointment if you are concerned
